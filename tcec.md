### 8\. In case your engine uses Training Data (NNUE or NN parts): what is the origin of this data? Did you obtain permission for use from the original authors if applicable?

**All training data is self-generated.** The pipeline plays MCTS games
of the current network vs Stockfish; positions, MCTS visit
distributions, and Stockfish WDL/MultiPV evaluations are written into a
disk-backed replay buffer (`chess\_anti\_engine/replay/`) and consumed by
the trainer. **No external training data, NNUE files, or pre-trained
network weights are imported.** The Stockfish evaluations recorded in
the buffer are produced at training time by a locally-run Stockfish
executable supplied by the operator — analogous to using Stockfish as
an oracle, not redistributing its data.

No permission is required because no third-party training data is
used.

### 10\. Does your engine comply with the unofficial TCEC NNUE Guideline, to the best of your knowledge? If so please tell us why you believe it does?

**DeepFin is not an NNUE engine.** It uses a transformer (\~15 M
parameters at the production config; capable of scaling to \~105 M with
`configs/default.yaml`) evaluated on GPU via PyTorch, in the Lc0 family
rather than the Stockfish/NNUE family. The TCEC NNUE Guideline (which
governs reuse of NNUE weights / SF-derived evaluation files) therefore
does not directly apply. To the spirit of the rule: **no NNUE weights
of any provenance are bundled or required**, and the network is
trained from scratch on self-generated data (see question 8).

### 11\. Describe the unique features of your engine, what is with it that no other engines do, or how no other engines work, or was invented for it before others employed a similar thing too?

Distinctive design points:

* **Anti-engine training premise.** The whole pipeline is explicitly
designed to *exploit Stockfish weaknesses* — fortress blindness,
horizon effects, overconfidence in closed positions — rather than to
learn a generally strong policy. The opponent is always Stockfish (at
PID-controlled handicap), not selfplay against the network's own past
versions, which is the opposite of the AlphaZero/Lc0 paradigm.
* **PID-controlled adaptive Stockfish difficulty using WDL-regret as
the primary lever.** A controller (`chess\_anti\_engine/stockfish/pid.py`)
targets \~60 % winrate by adjusting how much WDL regret Stockfish is
permitted per move (i.e. how suboptimally it plays), with node count
and skill level as secondary knobs. An "inverse-model" controller
variant replaced classical PID for the regret signal in April 2026.
* **Blended hard / soft WDL training of a single value head.** The main
WDL head is trained *simultaneously* on the hard game outcome and on
Stockfish's soft WDL eval (`w\_wdl + w\_sf\_wdl`). The two targets
disagree in \~63 % of samples, and that disagreement — selfplay outcome
vs. objective eval — is the signal that injects search-horizon
bootstrap into self-play games against a handicapped opponent.
* **Four parallel policy heads with distinct training targets.**
`policy\_own` (move actually played), `policy\_soft` (MCTS visit
distribution), `policy\_sf` (Stockfish MultiPV-derived soft target),
and `policy\_future` (predict-own-reply at t+2) all share the trunk
but are separately learned `AttentionPolicyHead`s with their own
Q/K/underpromo projections and a learnable temperature scalar each.
* **Multi-task auxiliary heads:** `sf\_eval` (separate SF-WDL channel),
`categorical` (HL-Gauss distributional value), `volatility` /
`sf\_volatility`, `moves\_left`.
* **Distributed selfplay with shared-memory inference broker.**
`inference.py:SharedSlotBroker` pre-allocates pinned-CPU shared
memory slots so worker processes hand positions to a single GPU
inference server with zero-copy GPU transfer.
* **Salvage workflow.** Past best checkpoints + replay shards are
exported as a "pool" and used as a one-shot warm-start seed for fresh
PBT trials, decoupling architectural restarts from data loss.
* **GPBT-pairwise** scheduler on top of Ray Tune for hyperparameter
exploration (currently pinned in production).

### 14\. Is there any other information you think is relevant and is missing from your replies above?

#### Training sketch (one outer iteration)

1. **Distributed self-play vs Stockfish.** Worker processes play
batches of MCTS games. The network's turn uses Gumbel MCTS over the
current weights; Stockfish plays its turn at a node count and
per-move *WDL-regret* budget set by the adaptive PID controller
(target \~60 % network winrate). MultiPV is enabled so every
Stockfish move yields a soft policy target plus a WDL eval. All
workers share one GPU via the shared-memory inference broker
(pre-allocated pinned-CPU slots, zero-copy GPU transfer).
2. **Shard upload.** Workers write positions to Zarr shards: 146-plane
input, played-move 1-hot, MCTS visit distribution, predict-own-reply
at t+2, SF MultiPV soft policy, SF WDL eval, eventual game outcome,
plus KataGo-style auxiliary signals (volatility, plies-to-end).
3. **Replay ingest.** Server-side compaction folds new shards into a
disk-backed sliding-window replay buffer; surprise weighting drives
sampling priority.
4. **Training step.** Trainer samples a batch and computes the
ten-headed loss (Q11) in BF16 mixed precision; AdamW with z-clip
gradient clipping.
5. **Publish + iterate.** New checkpoint goes to the workers, the PID
updates regret/nodes from the iteration's measured winrate, and the
loop repeats. PBT/GPBT-pairwise sits on top of this, with a salvage
workflow that warm-starts fresh trials from past best checkpoints +
replay.

#### Contrast with comparable engines

* **vs Lc0** — Lc0 is pure self-play, \~191 M params, C++ with custom
CUDA backends. DeepFin is anti-engine training (always vs handicapped
Stockfish), \~15 M params, Python/PyTorch with C extensions.
Architecturally Lc0-BT4-inspired (Smolgen, gating, 112 history
planes, 4672 policy) but reimplemented from scratch — and extended
with 34 added classical planes, four parallel policy heads, and a
blended hard/soft WDL value target rather than Lc0's single policy
head and pure outcome value.
* **vs Ceres** — Ceres is a C# search engine that hosts external
networks (Lc0, NNUE). DeepFin is a self-contained Python+CUDA+C
training-and-inference stack with its own Gumbel-MCTS search and its
own network format. DeepFin can export ONNX in a Ceres-compatible
layout (`chess\_anti\_engine/onnx/`), so Ceres could in principle host
a DeepFin net, but the engine does not depend on Ceres for search.
* **Conceptual borrowings from KataGo** (no source copied) — growing
sliding-window replay (not AlphaZero's fixed last-N-games shape);
surprise-weighted sampling so the trainer revisits hard positions;
auxiliary heads (volatility, moves-left) that share the trunk and
stabilize representation learning; latest-vs-best net-gating
scaffolding (currently disabled in production).

#### Operational notes

* Standard UCI; the handshake is emitted before model load completes
(Lc0 pattern), and `readyok` blocks until the engine is actually
ready.
* **`Ponder` defaults to off** and supports being left off.
* GPU-only in practice. Tuned on an NVIDIA RTX 5090. The UCI binary
loads via `DirectGPUEvaluator` with `torch.compile` + CUDA-graph
warmup; \~5–10 s startup finishes before the first `go`.
* Syzygy 3-4-5 WDL probing supported (`SyzygyPath`,
`Syzygy50MoveRule`).
* UCI options `Threads`, `MaxBatch`, `MinibatchSize`, `MultiPV` are
live-configurable. Defaults: 2 walker threads, 1024 max batch — the
proven single-GPU sweet spot.
